Shifters have one function, to move cable. Early shifters were very simple in that they featured a spool that relied on friction to hold their position against derailleur spring tension, and a lever that the rider could use to turn the spool. Since there was no built-in indexing feature, the rider essentially had to turn the lever until the chain pulled the desired gear, then manually micro-adjust the lever to get rid of any excessive noise and vibration. As shifters evolved, index points were introduced, then ratcheting mechanisms were incorporated to produce automatic indexing and allow the lever to return to its starting position after use. Modern shifters are available with a number of actuation options. Some types are actuated with fingers, thumbs, or both. Some allow the rider to twist a section of the grip to move cable. Some are built into brake levers, or adjacent to them. Some require only one lever to both pull and release cable. Some continue to use a similar method of actuation as early designs, albeit with a significant degree of refinement. And electronic shifters can be actuated in just about any manner you can think of. For cable shifters, the base requirements in order to work with the derailleurs are that they move the appropriate amount of cable to work with the derailleur’s actuation ratio, the appropriate amount to move the derailleur as far as it needs to move per shift, and have an equal amount of index points as there are the number of cogs on the cassette or chainrings on the crankset. Electronic shifters can be programmed to accomplish all of these things. Aside from these requirements, shifter design can address ergonomics, placement on the bike, friction reduction, durability, and weight.
