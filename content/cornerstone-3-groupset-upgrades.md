## Introduction

The groupset is your bike's nervous system — it moves you, stops you, and determines how the bike feels under load. And it's also the most expensive thing to upgrade.

Most cyclists who come to me asking about a groupset upgrade don't actually need one. They need a service, some new cables, and a proper gear adjustment.

But sometimes — genuinely — upgrading the groupset is the right call. This guide will help you figure out which situation you're in.

I say this as someone who earns a living doing groupset upgrades. If I can save you £500 by telling you a service is the better option, I'd rather do that. A bike with a cheap groupset that's properly maintained will always outperform a bike with an expensive groupset that's neglected.

So let's start with the honest answer: do you actually need a new groupset?

## Do You Actually Need a New Groupset?

Before anything else — answer these three questions honestly:

**1. Is your current groupset causing you a specific, named problem?**
Generic "it doesn't feel right" isn't a groupset problem. Specific problems like "my rear derailleur jumps on the 4th cog" or "my front derailleur won't shift to the big ring" are.

**2. Have you recently had a full service?**
If your bike hasn't been serviced in 6 months and your shifting is poor — the groupset isn't the problem. A service costs £135-165. A groupset upgrade costs £400-1,200. Service first.

**3. Is your frame worth upgrading?**
If your frame is worth £500 or less, a groupset upgrade rarely makes financial sense. The bike's value should justify the investment. If you have a £2,000+ frame with a Shimano 105 groupset, an upgrade to Ultegra or GRX 800 makes sense. If you have a £600 entry-level bike, sell it and buy a better one.

## The Signs It's Time to Upgrade

These are the genuine indicators that a new groupset is the right call:

### ✅ 1. Your Current Groupset Is Genuinely Worn

Chains, cassettes, and chainrings wear out. When they're worn, they stop shifting well even with a perfect tune-up. If your shop says "the drivetrain is worn beyond saving" — that's a legitimate upgrade reason.

**Signs of wear:**
- Chain skips even after a new chain
- Cassette teeth look shark-tooth sharp
- Chainring teeth are visibly worn (pointed rather than square)

### ✅ 2. You're Upgrading Your Frame

If you're putting a new frame on an old groupset, it usually makes more sense to upgrade the groupset too. Mixing old and new components can create compatibility issues.

### ✅ 3. Your Riding Has Changed Significantly

If you've moved from pure road to gravel riding, your road bike groupset might genuinely be wrong for the job. A GRX groupset on a road bike frame is a legitimate upgrade for a gravel conversion.

### ✅ 4. You Want Electronic Shifting

If you've ridden electronic shifting and want it — that's a valid reason. It's genuinely better shifting, and if the budget allows, it's worth it.

### ❌ Common Reasons That Are NOT Good Enough

- "I want better gears" (without a specific gear problem)
- "My mate has it and it looks better"
- "I want something lighter"
- "It would make my bike faster" (a worn £500 groupset won't make you faster; a £100 service might)

## What Is a Groupset and What Does It Include?

A groupset is the complete set of components that do three jobs: shift gears, stop the bike, and transmit power through the drivetrain.

**A complete groupset includes:**

**Gear shifting:**
- Brake levers (integrated with shift levers on road/gravel bikes)
- Front derailleur (for 2× and 3× setups)
- Rear derailleur

**Braking:**
- Brake calipers (mechanical or hydraulic disc)
- Brake rotors

**Drivetrain:**
- Crankset (chainrings + crank arms)
- Bottom bracket
- Chain
- Cassette (the rear sprockets)
- Pedals (technically not part of the groupset but included by most people)

**What isn't included:**
- Handlebars
- Stem
- Seatpost
- Saddle
- Wheels

## Shimano GRX — The Gravel Standard

Shimano GRX is the default groupset for gravel bikes in the UK. It's excellent, widely serviced, and comes in a clear hierarchy.

### GRX 400 — Entry Gravel

**Gears:** 2×10 speed
**Brakes:** Mechanical disc
**Price tier:** Budget

**Our view:** Fine for the price, but mechanical disc brakes on a gravel bike are a compromise. If you're spending money on an upgrade, we'd recommend starting at GRX 600.

---

### GRX 600 — The Sweet Spot

**Gears:** 2×11 speed
**Brakes:** Hydraulic disc
**Price tier:** Mid-range

**This is what we recommend for most gravel builds.**

Hydraulic disc brakes are genuinely superior to mechanical — more power, better modulation, self-adjusting. The 2×11 gearing gives you excellent range for mixed terrain.

**What you get for the extra money over GRX 400:**
- Hydraulic brakes (£80-120 value)
- Better front derailleur
- More precise shifting
- Wider range of cassette options

---

### GRX 800 — Premium Mechanical

**Gears:** 2×11 speed
**Brakes:** Hydraulic disc
**Price tier:** Premium

**Our view:** The best mechanical gravel groupset Shimano makes. Excellent shifting, powerful brakes, reliable. If you're building a premium steel or titanium gravel bike, this is the groupset to specify.

**Upgrade from GRX 600:** Worth it if you're a serious rider, not worth it if you're a recreational cyclist.

---

### GRX 810 Di2 — Premium Electronic

**Gears:** 2×11 speed electronic
**Brakes:** Hydraulic disc
**Price tier:** Premium +

**Our view:** The best gravel groupset on the market for most people. Electronic shifting is noticeably smoother, especially on the front derailleur. If budget allows and you're building a premium bike, this is the groupset.

**Is electronic worth the premium?** Yes — if your total build budget is £3,000 or more. Below that, mechanical shifting is more than good enough.

---

## SRAM — The Simpler Option

SRAM offers a different philosophy: simpler, lighter, often wireless.

### Apex — Entry Level

**Gears:** 1×12 speed
**Brakes:** Hydraulic disc
**Price tier:** Budget

**The 1× advantage:** No front derailleur means simpler shifting and less weight. The wide-range 12-speed cassette gives you similar range to 2× setups.

**The 1× disadvantage:** If a chainring fails on a 1×, you have no backup. On 2×, you can usually get home on the small ring.

**Our view:** Good for budget builds. Not our first choice for serious gravel riding.

---

### Rival — Mid-Range

**Gears:** 2×12 speed
**Brakes:** Hydraulic disc
**Price tier:** Mid-range

**Our view:** SRAM's answer to GRX 600. Excellent groupset, different ergonomics to Shimano (the brake lever shape is different). Worth trying before you buy.

---

### Force AXS — Premium Wireless

**Gears:** 2×12 speed wireless electronic
**Brakes:** Hydraulic disc
**Price tier:** Premium

**Our view:** The wireless shifting revolution is real. No cables, no fiddling, instant shifts. Force AXS is significantly more expensive than Shimano GRX 810 Di2 but the wireless advantage is real — especially on a custom build where cable routing is always a challenge.

**If budget is no object:** This is the groupset to get.

---

## Campagnolo Ekar — The Italian Choice

Ekar is Campagnolo's gravel groupset. 1×13 speed, mechanical or electronic, beautiful build quality.

**Our view:** Technically excellent. But Campagnolo is harder to service in the UK — fewer mechanics work on it, parts take longer to source. We wouldn't specify it unless the customer specifically wanted it.

## Electronic vs Mechanical Shifting — Is It Worth It?

The short answer: **yes, if you can afford it without stretching your budget elsewhere.**

The long answer:

### Why Electronic Is Better

1. **Precision** — Every shift is exact, every time. No cable stretch, no lever travel variation.
2. **Consistency** — Shifting feels the same in January as it does in July.
3. **Simplicity** — No gear cables to route or maintain.
4. **Front derailleur** — Electronic front shifts are instant and perfect. Mechanical front shifts on a 2× can be inconsistent.
5. **Aesthetics** — Clean lines, no cables.

### Why Mechanical Is Fine

1. **Cost** — Electronic adds £400-1,200 to a build. That's a significant part of the budget.
2. **Servicing** — Electronic groupsets need specialist service. Not every mechanic can work on Di2 or AXS.
3. **Durability** — Electronic has more electronic components that could theoretically fail. In practice, both are very reliable.
4. **It's already very good** — GRX 800 mechanical shifts beautifully. For 95% of riders, the difference between mechanical and electronic is unnoticeable in real-world riding.

**Our recommendation:** Get mechanical shifting if you're on a budget. Get electronic if you're building a £4,000+ bike and the difference between £3,000 and £4,000 isn't a stretch.

## The Labour Cost Question

Labour is where the real variation happens. Here's what affects it:

### Factors That Increase Labour

**1. Frame compatibility issues**
Modern gravel frames often use T47 threaded bottom brackets or press-fit (BB86/BB92). If your frame is an older standard or an unusual size, fitting takes longer.

**2. Electronic groupset wiring**
Routing electronic wires through a frame can be surprisingly time-consuming, especially on frames without internal routing ports.

**3. Brake bleeding**
Hydraulic brakes need bleeding after installation. Budget 30-60 minutes for this.

**4. Derailleur hanger alignment**
Most new derailleurs require a hanger alignment check — bent hangers cause shifting problems. This adds 15-30 minutes.

### Typical Labour Costs

| Job | Labour Time | Approx Cost |
|-----|------------|------------|
| Mechanical groupset install (new frame) | 3-5 hours | £150–£250 |
| Mechanical groupset swap (same frame) | 2-3 hours | £100–£180 |
| Electronic groupset install | 4-6 hours | £200–£350 |
| Hydraulic brake bleed | 30-60 mins | £40–£80 |
| Single derailleur replacement | 1-2 hours | £50–£100 |

## Can Any Mechanic Install a New Groupset?

Short answer: no.

Longer answer:

### What Anyone Can Do
Basic mechanical groupset swaps — where you're replacing like with like on a standard frame — are achievable for competent home mechanics. YouTube has excellent tutorials.

### What Requires a Specialist
- Electronic groupset wiring and setup
- Frames with unusual bottom bracket standards
- Hydraulic brake installation and bleeding
- Carbon frame preparation (torque settings are critical)
- First-time installation of a different brand groupset on an existing frame

### How to Check If Your Mechanic Knows What They're Doing

Ask them these questions before you commit:

1. "What bottom bracket standard does my frame use?" — If they can't answer this, walk away.
2. "Do you have experience with [your groupset]?" — e.g. Shimano GRX Di2 or SRAM AXS specifically.
3. "Do you charge per hour or flat rate?" — Flat rate is usually better for known jobs.
4. "Do you include a test ride?" — Professional mechanics always test ride after a groupset install.
