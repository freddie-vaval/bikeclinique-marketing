## Why Build Custom Instead of Buying Off the Rack?

Off-the-rack bikes are excellent. The best bang-for-buck in the history of cycling. A £1,500 Specialized or Canyon will outperform 90% of custom builds from 10 years ago.

But there are three situations where custom makes more sense:

### 1. You Have Specific Requirements Production Bikes Can't Meet

Large hands need different brake levers. Long torsos need a longer top tube. Saddle height and reach interact in ways that production geometry can't always solve.

If you've been fitted and know your exact stack and reach numbers, a custom frame lets you build to those exact measurements.

### 2. You Want a Specific Use Case

Most production gravel bikes are compromises — trying to be good at fire roads, fast on tarmac, light enough for trails.

If your riding is 90% unmade roads and 10% everything else, you don't need the tarmac compromises a production gravel bike makes. A custom build can be optimised for your actual use.

### 3. You Want to Learn How Your Bike Works

Building a custom bike teaches you more about cycling mechanics in one project than five years of riding. When you've built it, you understand it. When you understand it, you maintain it better.

## The 5 Decisions That Define Your Build

Every custom gravel bike build comes down to five choices. Everything else is detail.

1. **Frame material** — Steel, carbon, or titanium
2. **Frame geometry** — Racing geometry vs endurance vs adventure
3. **Groupset** — The shifting, braking, and drivetrain
4. **Wheels** — The most overlooked part of any build
5. **Finishing kit** — Bars, stem, saddle, droppers

Get these five right and the rest is commentary.

## Frame Material: Steel vs Carbon vs Titanium

This is where most first-time custom builders get stuck. Here's the honest breakdown.

### Steel

**The classic.** Steel frames have been the benchmark for 80 years and they're not going anywhere.

**Pros:**
- Relatively affordable
- Repairable anywhere in the world (just find a welder)
- Comfortable ride quality — steel soaks up road buzz
- Classic aesthetic
- Easy to work on — standard sizing, threaded bottom brackets

**Cons:**
- Heavier than carbon or titanium (but not by as much as people think)
- Can rust if not maintained

**Our recommendation for:** Riders on a budget who want maximum repairability and a classic feel. Our most popular custom builds are steel.

**Budget for frame:** £400–£1,200 for a quality UK-built steel frame.

---

### Carbon

**The performance choice.** Modern carbon frames are extraordinary pieces of engineering.

**Pros:**
- Significant weight saving
- Stiff where you need it, compliant where you want comfort
- Huge range of geometries from hundreds of manufacturers
- Aerodynamic options available

**Cons:**
- Expensive to repair (not a field repair)
- More expensive to manufacture — higher base cost
- Quality varies hugely between manufacturers
- You need to trust your builder with expensive layup

**Our recommendation for:** Riders who prioritise performance and have the budget for it. Also good if you're building a dedicated race bike where every watt matters.

**Budget for frame:** £800–£3,000 depending on manufacturer.

---

### Titanium

**The forever choice.** A titanium frame is a lifetime purchase.

**Pros:**
- Doesn't rust or fatigue
- Comfortable and stiff in the right proportions
- Lifetime frame warranty from most manufacturers
- Exceptional resale value

**Cons:**
- Most expensive option
- Requires specialised welding equipment
- Limited paint options — raw titanium or anodised only

**Our recommendation for:** Riders who view the bike as a long-term investment and want the best all-round ride quality. Titanium is the benchmark for a reason.

**Budget for frame:** £1,500–£5,000 for a UK-built titanium gravel frame.

### The Geometry Question: How Do You Want It to Ride?

This is more important than the material.

**Racing geometry** — Longer top tube, shorter head tube, lower stack. More aggressive, faster on roads, twitchier on trails.

**Endurance geometry** — Shorter top tube, taller head tube, higher stack. More comfortable, more stable on long rides, slightly slower on roads.

**Adventure geometry** — Slack head angle, long wheelbase, maximum tyre clearance. Built for technical terrain, slower on roads, extraordinary on trails.

For South London use — which is a mix of asphalt, canal paths, and occasional off-road — we typically recommend **endurance-adventure geometry**. Fast enough on the commute, confident on the trails, comfortable all day.

## The Groupset: Your Most Important Decision

The groupset is the most expensive single component and the part that determines how your bike actually shifts and stops.

For gravel bikes in 2026, there are four real options:

### Shimano GRX

The default choice for most UK gravel builds. GRX is excellent, widely available, and easy to service.

| Model | Gears | Brake Type | Price Tier |
|-------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| GRX 400 | 2×10 | Mechanical | Budget |
| GRX 600 | 2×11 | Hydraulic | Mid |
| GRX 800 | 2×11 | Hydraulic | Premium |
| GRX 810 Di2 | 2×11 | Hydraulic | Premium + electronic |

**Our view:** GRX 600 is the sweet spot for most builds. Hydraulic brakes are non-negotiable for serious gravel — mechanical discs are a false economy. We wouldn't build a serious gravel bike with anything less than GRX 600.

**Budget for groupset (frame, fork, headset, bars, saddle):** £450–£700 for GRX 600.

---

### SRAM Rival / Force / Apex

SRAM's answer to GRX. Excellent groupsets with slightly different ergonomics.

| Model | Gears | Brake Type | Price Tier |
|-------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Apex | 1×12 | Hydraulic | Mid |
| Rival | 2×12 | Hydraulic | Mid |
| Force | 2×12 | Hydraulic | Premium |
| Force AXS | 2×12 | Wireless | Premium |

**Our view:** SRAM's 1× setups are simpler and lighter but have a narrower gear range. If you're doing big climbs regularly, the 2× options make more sense. The wireless AXS shifting is genuinely impressive — but at a significant price premium.

---

### Campagnolo Ekar

The Italian option. Ekar is a 1×13 groupset — technically impressive but harder to source and service in the UK.

**Our view:** Excellent if you're committed to Campagnolo's ecosystem. Otherwise GRX is more practical for UK-based servicing.

### The Compatibility Question (Most People Get This Wrong)

Before you buy anything, check:

1. **Bottom bracket standard** — Most modern gravel frames use T47 (threaded) or PF86. Check what your frame uses before buying cranks.

2. **Front derailleur mount** — If you're running 2×, check your frame has a front derailleur mount or dedicated chainline clearance for 2× chainring setups.

3. **Brake mount** — Flat mount is standard for modern gravel frames. Make sure your caliper is flat mount, not post mount (unless you're running an adapter).

4. **Thru-axle spec** — 12mm front and rear is standard. Check your wheel hub width matches your frame.

We check all of this in every build consultation. If you're unsure, ask before you buy — returns on groupsets are not fun.

## Wheels — Where Most People Go Wrong

This is where most first-time builders underinvest.

A great frame with mediocre wheels will feel mediocre. A decent frame with excellent wheels will feel brilliant.

### What Makes a Good Gravel Wheel?

**Hub quality** — The most important factor. Good hubs last 20,000km+ with basic maintenance. Cheap hubs wear out in 5,000km and develop play that affects shifting and braking.

**Spoke count and lacing** — 28-32 spokes for a gravel build. More spokes = more durable. Avoid 24-spoke wheels on a bike you'll ride on rough terrain.

**Rim width** — For gravel, you want an internal rim width of 23-25mm minimum. This gives better tyre profile and puncture resistance. Narrow rims on wide tyres are a gravel bike sin.

**Tubeless compatibility** — Non-negotiable for gravel. Tubeless tyres seal automatically when they puncture, letting you continue riding instead of stopping to fix a tube.

**Brake type** — Disc brake wheels need disc-compatible rims (or hubs). Make sure your wheel set matches your frame's brake standard.
