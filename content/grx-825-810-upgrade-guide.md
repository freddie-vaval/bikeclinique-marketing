## Introduction

The GRX 810 has been the default choice for serious gravel riders for a few years now. It hit the sweet spot — electronic shifting, 2×11 speeds, hydraulic disc brakes, and a price that didn't require a second mortgage.

Then Shimano launched the GRX 825 Di2.

If you're running GRX 810 and wondering whether to upgrade — or you're spec'ing a new custom gravel build and deciding between the two — here's the honest answer.

Spoiler: most people don't need to upgrade. But some people absolutely should.

---

## What Is GRX 825 Di2?

GRX 825 Di2 is Shimano's latest iteration of the GRX platform — positioned above GRX 810 and below the top-tier GRX 920 Di2.

It's a **wired 2×12-speed electronic groupset** with Bluetooth connectivity built in. That means you can connect your phone, GPS unit, or power meter without additional dongles.

## What Actually Changed

### 1. 12-Speed (2×12)

The jump from 11 to 12 speeds sounds cosmetic. It isn't.

More speeds means **finer gear steps** — especially useful on the UK's varied terrain. You lose less momentum when the gradient changes. On a long gravel ride in the South Downs or the Lakes, those smaller jumps between gears add up.

The 12-speed also means Shimano could add a **46/29 double crankset** alongside the standard 48/31. If you live somewhere hilly — and most of South London running into Kent, Surrey, and Sussex is rolling at minimum — that easier bottom gear genuinely matters.

### 2. Refined Shifting

The front derailleur shift feels smoother on the 825. This isn't marketing fluff — the earlier GRX Di2 units had a slightly clunky FD shift compared to the rear. The 825 fixes that.

### 3. Brake Lever Feel

The lever reach adjustment is easier and the ergonomics are marginally better for all-day comfort. Not a reason to upgrade. But noticeable if you're setting up a new bike.

## Who Should Buy GRX 825 Di2 (And Who Shouldn't)

### Buy it if:
- You're building a new gravel bike and spec'ing from scratch — the 825 is the better platform
- You live somewhere genuinely hilly and want the 46/29 option
- You're coming from mechanical shifting and want the smoothest possible electronic experience
- You want the wider gear range for mixed-terrain riding

### Don't bother if:
- You already own GRX 810 and it's working well — the upgrade cost doesn't justify the gains
- You're on a budget — GRX 810 is still an excellent groupset and significantly cheaper
- You want wireless — look at GRX 827 instead

If you're getting a custom gravel build from us:

## The Honest Verdict

GRX 825 Di2 is a genuine improvement over 810 — particularly the 12-speed and the 46/29 crankset option. But it's not a night-and-day difference.

**For new builds:** Choose 825 and get the wider range.
**For upgrades from 810:** Stay on 810 until it breaks or you sell the bike.
**For budget builds:** GRX 810 is still the best value in the Shimano gravel range.
