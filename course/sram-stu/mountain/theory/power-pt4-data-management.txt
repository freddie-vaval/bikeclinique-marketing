Once the power meter measures torque and cadence and converts that information into a power reading, that reading is wirelessly transmitted to the cycling computer. In addition to powering the measuring components and microcontrollers, the battery in the power meter also powers a signal transmitter. The transmitter generates radio waves that are picked up by a receiver in the cycling computer. The two most common transmission protocols for fitness products are ANT+ and Bluetooth Low Energy, or BLE. ANT+ transmits information at a unique radio frequency and allows the power meter to communicate with any ANT+ cycling computer that is paired with the power meter. As the name implies, BLE functions similarly to traditional Bluetooth, but does so with a lower energy requirement and at a lower cost. BLE also operates at a unique radio frequency, but only allows for one single cycling computer to be connected to the power meter at a given time. The most common cycling computer type is the head unit. Head units are mounted to the handlebar or stem for easy access and viewing. Some cycling computers can be worn on the wrist, although these aren’t as easy to monitor. Cycling computers can allow the rider to view their power output in real-time. They can also store data, allowing it to be downloaded to a separate computer and analyzed using specialized software. This can allow the rider to measure, monitor, and compare their performance with other rides or other riders. Cadence information can also be received and viewed separately from wattage. A variety of other information is available with onboard computers, depending on the features available per unit. That’s it for power. For more information on power meters, be sure to visit sram.com.
